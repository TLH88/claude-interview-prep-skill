§ 01

The company, in context

Cobalt sells payment and ledger infrastructure to other fintechs. The rails are mature and trusted. The data layer on top of them is not, and that is the opening. This role exists because Cobalt's customers now want insight, not just plumbing, and someone has to turn raw transaction data into a product they will pay more for.

C

What Cobalt is now

Series C payments-and-ledger infrastructure for fintechs and platforms. The core rails are stable and trusted. The bet now is moving up the stack from plumbing into analytics and decisioning that customers will pay a premium for.

Small, autonomous product squads. Written specs and async review carry the decisions, not meetings. ~350 people, remote-first across US time zones. Devin's org owns the data-products line. Your Flowstate squad ran the same way: written-first, pod-aligned.

Q3
Tell me about a product you took from zero in an ambiguous spot.
STAR
▾
Situation
Flowstate had a powerful workflow engine but customers only saw their own data as flat exports. Leadership wanted a self-serve analytics product, but there was no spec, no clear buyer, and an assumption that "more dashboards" was the answer.
Task
Define and ship a product that got customers to a useful insight on their own, fast enough that they'd pay for it, without a playbook to copy.
Action
Started from the activation problem, not the feature list. Instrumented where new accounts stalled and found the wall was time-to-first-insight: three days of setup before anyone saw value. Reframed the product around collapsing that. Shipped opinionated default views instead of a blank dashboard builder, wired in templated insights for the top three jobs, and ran a weekly discovery loop with design and data science. Made the call to cut configurability in v1 to protect speed-to-value.
Result
Time-to-first-insight went from 3 days to 4 hours. Activation rose 22% → 38%. The product reached $4.2M ARR in 18 months. The right default beats the flexible blank slate when the goal is first value.
Q4
How do you use AI in the product, and what did you build vs. buy?
High priority
▾
Why it matters
Cobalt just shipped an AI feature, so Devin will probe whether you reason about AI like an owner or a consumer. Your edge is the eval discipline, not the model choice. Lead with how you decided and how you measured, then the outcome.
Bought (and why)
For the LLM itself, we bought: a hosted frontier model via API. Building or fine-tuning our own made no sense for plain-language summaries of dashboards, where the differentiation is the data and the guardrails, not the weights.
Built (the part that mattered)
We built the layer around the model: a retrieval step that grounded every summary in the customer's actual metrics, a prompt and schema that forced cited numbers, and an eval harness that scored each release for hallucination rate and numeric accuracy against a labeled set before it shipped. I set the bar that we would not ship a summary feature until measured hallucination on numbers was under our threshold.
Result & closing
The summaries shipped, were trusted because the numbers were right, and contributed to the week-4 retention lift on Signal. The model is a commodity. The judgment is deciding what to build around it and how you'll know it's safe to ship. That's the part I own.
Q5
How do you think about activation and time-to-first-value?
High priority
▾
Frame
This is Devin's stated hardest problem, so this is the answer to over-prepare. Define first value concretely, then show the loop you run to shorten the path to it. Anchor every claim in the Flowstate numbers.
How I work it
I define one first-value moment per product and make it measurable, not a vibe. At Flowstate it was "sees an insight that's true about their own data." Then I instrument the path to it, find the single biggest stall, and remove it before adding anything new. The discipline is refusing to add features until the activation wall is down, because new surface area usually moves the wall further away.
Proof
That approach moved time-to-first-insight from 3 days to 4 hours and activation from 22% to 38%. Same product, same model, mostly removal rather than addition. For Cobalt I'd want to know what your defined first-value moment is today, and whether it's instrumented yet.
Q6
Tell me about leading your squad through competing priorities or conflict.
STAR
▾
Situation
Mid-build on the analytics product, Sales pushed hard for a custom export feature for one large prospect while my squad was heads-down on the activation work that the data said mattered more for everyone.
Task
Hold the roadmap on what the evidence supported without torching the relationship with Sales or the deal.
Action
Didn't argue in the abstract. Put the activation funnel data next to the one-deal request and showed the squad and Sales the same numbers. Offered a smaller, reusable version of the export that served the prospect and the broader base, and time-boxed it. Made the tradeoff visible instead of making it a turf fight.
Result
The prospect got an export that shipped to everyone, the activation work stayed on track, and Sales started bringing me funnel data instead of feature demands. The disagreement became a shared decision.
Q7
A time discovery changed your direction.
STAR
▾
Situation
We had a "report builder" committed for the quarter. It was the obvious-sounding answer to "customers want more from their data," and it had executive support.
Task
Validate it was the right build before sinking a quarter of eng into it.
Action
Ran 9 discovery interviews with active and churned accounts. The pattern was clear: customers didn't want to build reports, they wanted to be told what mattered without building anything. Brought the recordings and a tight summary to leadership and recommended killing the report builder in favor of the insights layer.
Result
We cut it and redirected the quarter to Signal, the insights layer, which drove the 15-point week-4 retention lift. Killing the wrong build was the highest-leverage decision of the quarter.
Q8
How do you partner with GTM on pricing and packaging?
Domain
▾
Frame
Cobalt wants to charge a premium for the insight layer, so pricing is part of this PM job, not someone else's. Show you've done it, not just opined on it.
Script
I treat pricing as a product decision made with GTM, not handed to them. For Flowstate's analytics tier I co-built usage-based pricing with Sales and Finance: we anchored the metric to the value customers actually got (insights surfaced, not seats), modeled it against real account usage, and pressure-tested edge cases where the metric could punish a good customer. I defended it in two QBRs when expansion came in under plan and we adjusted the tier boundaries based on where accounts actually sat. The principle: price the value moment, then watch the data and correct.
