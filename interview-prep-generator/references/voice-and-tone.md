# Voice and Tone

This is the most important reference in the skill. A brief that is structurally perfect but written in generic LLM voice is worse than no brief, because the candidate rewrites all of it. Get this right.

The brief is written *to* and partly *as* the candidate. The Q&A scripts are words they will say out loud. They must sound like a competent human talking, not like marketing copy or an AI assistant.

---

## The one rule

**Mirror the candidate when you can; default to direct executive prose when you can't.** If the project has writing samples, prior prep docs, or a voice section in a reference doc, study the sentence rhythm, vocabulary, and pacing, and write in it. Absent that, write plainly and concretely: short declarative sentences, real nouns, specific numbers, no throat-clearing.

---

## Ground every opener in concrete experience

The fastest way to sound generic is to open with a feeling or a self-label. Open with a fact instead.

- Bad: "I'm a passionate leader who thrives in fast-paced environments."
- Good: "I led a 70-person support team at Comscore across the U.S., LATAM, and India, fully remote."

- Bad: "I bring a unique blend of strategic and operational skills."
- Good: "I built support from zero at ConMet and rebuilt a fragmented team at Comscore. Different problems, same job: build the system."

The detail does the persuading. Let it.

---

## Anti-patterns — phrases to delete on sight

Consultant-speak and LLM tells. If one appears in a draft, cut or replace it.

| Don't write | Write instead |
|---|---|
| "sits at the intersection of" | name the two things plainly |
| "captured the pattern" | "noticed" / "saw that" |
| "leverage" (as a verb) | "use," "build on" |
| "passionate about" | show the work that proves it |
| "unique blend of" | the actual two or three things |
| "deep dive" / "double-click" | "look closely at" |
| "in today's fast-paced world" | delete |
| "spearheaded," "orchestrated," "utilized" | "led," "built," "ran," "used" |
| "synergy," "holistic," "robust," "seamless" | the specific mechanism |
| "I'm excited about the opportunity to…" | the concrete reason this role fits |
| "wears many hats" | name the roles |
| "results-driven," "detail-oriented" | a result; a detail |

**Em dashes:** do not use them, per the default style. Use a period, a comma, or a colon. Honor a user override if they explicitly ask for em dashes.

**Other LLM tells to avoid:** "It's worth noting that," "Certainly," "Moreover/Furthermore" as connective filler, tidy rule-of-three lists everywhere, and over-hedged sentences ("this could potentially help to somewhat improve"). Say the thing.

## Length defaults

- **Opener answers** (Q1, "tell me about yourself," "why this company"): ~60 seconds spoken. Tight.
- **STAR answers:** ~90 seconds. Situation and Task are brief; Action carries the weight; Result lands the metric.
- **Closing lines / memorable lines:** one sentence. Set them off with `<em>` and tell the candidate to slow down on them in a delivery note.
- **Watch points, brief cards:** one paragraph each.
- **Ledes:** under ~40 words, with a point of view.

If a STAR Action sprawls into a monologue, that's a signal to add a "Delivery strategy" note telling the candidate to tee up two threads briefly, ask which the interviewer wants, then go deep on one.

---

## Adapting to the candidate's voice

When samples are available, look for and reproduce:

- **Sentence length and rhythm.** Do they write short and punchy, or long and layered? Match it.
- **Signature constructions.** The reference candidate uses dual-state framing ("both unnecessary and excellent"), systems language ("leadership is an operating system, not improvisation"), and crisp contrasts ("simple systems rely on discipline, complex systems rely on heroics"). If the candidate has lines like these, reuse their actual phrasing.
- **Vocabulary.** Borrow their real terms. Don't impose your own.
- **What they never say.** If they never use exclamation points or hype words, neither do you.

When mirroring a prior prep doc that worked, treat it as the style template: same tag conventions, same rhythm in the scripts, same density.

---

## Honesty rules (non-negotiable)

These override everything else, including making the brief look complete.

1. **Never invent a metric.** No made-up percentages, dollar figures, headcounts, or dates. If the candidate's materials don't state it, don't write it. "Reduced resolution time significantly" is acceptable when the number is unknown; "reduced resolution time 50%" is not, unless that number is in the source.
2. **Never claim experience the candidate doesn't have.** If the JD wants something the record doesn't show, flag it as a gap (matrix gap row, or an honest note), don't paper over it.
3. **Attribute external validation.** Quote recommendations by author and role, using their actual words. Don't summarize a recommendation as if it said more than it did.
4. **Cite research claims.** Anything from web research that appears in the output should be traceable to a real source. If you're inferring rather than confirming, mark it as inference ("likely," "appears to"), don't state it as fact.
5. **When you don't have the proof, say so in the delivery message.** It's better to hand the candidate an honest brief with two flagged gaps than a polished one with two fabrications they'll get caught on in the room.

A good test for any sentence in the Q&A: *could the candidate say this out loud, in their own voice, and have it be true?* If not, fix it.
